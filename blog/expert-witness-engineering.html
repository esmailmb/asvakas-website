Expert Witness Engineering: Testimony, Reports & Construction Litigation

When construction disputes go to court, structural engineering expert witnesses deliver the technical opinions that determine outcomes. Here's how the process works.

What Is an Engineering Expert Witness?

An engineering expert witness is a licensed professional engineer retained to provide technical opinions in legal proceedings — litigation, arbitration, insurance disputes, or regulatory hearings. Unlike a fact witness who testifies about what they directly observed, an expert witness is permitted to offer opinions: analyses, conclusions, and professional judgments based on their expertise. Courts recognize that fact-finders (judges and juries) cannot independently evaluate complex structural engineering questions, and expert witnesses bridge that gap.

Structural engineering expert witnesses appear in a wide range of matters. Their common thread is that the technical merits of the dispute turn on questions of structural engineering — questions of load capacity, code compliance, material behavior, construction practice, or professional standard of care that cannot be answered without specialized training.

Types of Cases Requiring Expert Engineers

  • Construction defect claims: Allegations that a contractor built defectively or that a designer's specifications were deficient
  • Design professional malpractice: Claims against architects or engineers for errors or omissions in design
  • Structural collapse incidents: Personal injury or property damage claims arising from partial or total collapse
  • Facade failure: Claims involving falling masonry, glass, or cladding causing personal injury or property damage
  • Construction contract disputes: Disagreements over changed conditions, scope, or completion status
  • Insurance claims: Coverage disputes involving building collapse, earth movement, or construction damage to adjacent properties
  • Government investigations: Agency inquiries following structural failures or safety violations

The Daubert Standard & FRE Rule 702

Under the Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals standard (1993), federal courts act as gatekeepers of expert testimony. Federal Rule of Evidence 702 requires that an expert's testimony be admitted only if:

  1. The expert has sufficient qualifications — knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education
  2. The testimony is based on sufficient facts or data
  3. The testimony is the product of reliable principles and methods
  4. The expert's opinions reflect a reliable application of those principles to the facts of the case

The Supreme Court's 1999 Kumho Tire v. Carmichael decision extended Daubert's gatekeeping role beyond scientific testimony to all expert testimony, including engineering. Most state courts have adopted similar standards (many through the Frye test or state-specific Daubert-like rules). A structural engineering expert who cannot articulate the methodology behind their opinions, or whose opinions are not grounded in generally accepted engineering practice, may be excluded entirely.

The Expert's Roles: Report, Deposition & Trial

The Expert Report

The written expert report is the foundation of the expert's work product. Under FRCP Rule 26(a)(2)(B), testifying experts in federal court must prepare a comprehensive report containing all opinions and their complete basis before the close of expert discovery. The report is the expert's primary communication — it must be comprehensive, well-reasoned, and stand on its own without supplementation at trial. Opinions not included in the report are generally not admissible.

Deposition

After report exchange, opposing counsel takes the expert's deposition under oath. Depositions test the scope, foundation, and limits of the expert's opinions. A skilled opposing attorney will probe for: opinions without adequate factual basis; methodology inconsistencies; reliance on incomplete evidence; internal inconsistencies between the report and prior statements; and any bias that suggests the expert is an advocate rather than an objective professional.

Trial Testimony

At trial, the expert first presents direct testimony explaining their opinions to the jury or judge. Direct examination focuses on establishing qualifications, the investigation process, and the opinions with clarity accessible to a non-technical fact-finder. Cross-examination probes the same vulnerabilities as deposition. An effective expert witness communicates complex technical concepts plainly, supports every opinion with evidence, acknowledges the limits of their certainty, and maintains professional composure.

Expert Report Contents (FRCP Rule 26)

A complete expert report under FRCP 26(a)(2)(B) must include:

  • A complete statement of all opinions and the basis for each
  • The facts and data considered in forming each opinion
  • All exhibits that will be used to support opinions at trial
  • The expert's qualifications, including publications in the past 10 years
  • A list of all cases in which the expert has testified in the past 4 years
  • The expert's compensation for the study and testimony

Independence & Objectivity

The defining attribute of an effective expert witness is independence. An expert who advocates for the retaining party — shading findings, overstating certainty, or suppressing inconvenient evidence — destroys the credibility their technical credentials are meant to provide. Courts, arbitrators, and sophisticated opposing counsel can identify advocacy masquerading as expertise.

The standard of professional conduct for expert engineering witnesses is clear: the expert's duty is to the court, not to the retaining party. Retaining counsel is entitled to a competent, thorough, and objective investigation — not a desired outcome. An expert who tells counsel only what they want to hear is not protecting the client; they are creating a liability.

How to Retain the Right Expert

Retain early — before litigation is filed when possible. Expert investigations require time: document review, site visits, testing, and analysis cannot be rushed without compromising quality. Key selection criteria:

  • PE licensure and expertise specifically matching the technical issues in dispute
  • Substantial practice experience in the relevant technical area
  • Prior court qualification in the relevant jurisdiction as an expert witness
  • Clear conflict check against adverse parties and counsel
  • Willingness to provide a complete, objective opinion — even if the findings do not favor the retaining party

Frequently Asked Questions

What qualifications does a structural engineering expert need?

Under Daubert/FRE Rule 702: specialized knowledge in the technical area at issue, PE licensure in the relevant jurisdiction, substantial relevant practice experience, and a methodology that can withstand legal scrutiny. Prior expert witness experience in similar cases is valuable but not required.

What is in an expert witness report?

Under FRCP Rule 26(a)(2)(B): all opinions and their complete basis, facts and data considered, exhibits to be used, the expert's qualifications and publications, prior testimony history, and compensation. State court requirements are generally similar.

What types of construction disputes use expert engineers?

Construction defect claims, design malpractice, structural collapse incidents, facade failure personal injury, construction contract disputes, insurance coverage disputes, and government investigations following safety violations.

What is the difference between a consulting and testifying expert?

A consulting expert advises counsel behind the scenes — their work is typically work-product protected. A testifying expert must be disclosed, produces a formal report under FRCP 26, and is subject to deposition and Daubert challenge. Separate consultating and testifying experts are sometimes retained in sensitive matters.
