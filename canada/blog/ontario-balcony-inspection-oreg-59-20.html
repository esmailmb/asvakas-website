Ontario Balcony Inspection: O. Reg. 59/20 Compliance Guide

Ontario's mandatory balcony inspection regulation imposes a 5-year inspection cycle on apartment and condo buildings across the province. Missed deadlines and deferred repairs expose building owners to serious liability. Here's the complete compliance guide.

Background: Why Ontario Enacted O. Reg. 59/20

Ontario's balcony inspection regulation — O. Reg. 59/20 under the Building Code Act, 1992 — was enacted in response to repeated incidents of balcony structural failures and falling concrete involving Ontario apartment and condominium buildings. Post-war concrete slab construction, saltwater exposure from winter de-icing agents, and decades of deferred maintenance had left many balconies across Ontario in various states of deterioration without mandatory inspection programs to detect and address this.

The regulation came into force on January 1, 2022, creating for the first time a province-wide legal requirement for periodic professional inspection of balconies. It parallels Ontario's existing requirements under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (RTA) and the Condominium Act, 1998 that require buildings to be maintained in a state of good repair — but makes the engineering inspection and documentation requirement explicit and mandatory.

Scope: Which Buildings and Which Structures

O. Reg. 59/20 applies to publicly accessible exterior structures on residential buildings 3 or more storeys in height, specifically:

  • Balconies — projecting horizontal platforms attached to the exterior of a building
  • Terraces — large outdoor platform areas on the roof level or other levels of a building
  • Exterior walkways — covered or uncovered horizontal circulation paths attached to the exterior of a building
  • Exterior exits — exterior exit stairways and landings attached to the building exterior

The regulation applies to both:

  • Condominium corporations — responsible for the common elements of their buildings, including shared balconies, terraces, and walkways
  • Rental apartment building owners — responsible for all balconies and exterior structures across their portfolio
📋

Important distinction: For condominium buildings, the regulation applies to the common element components of balconies (structure, waterproofing, railings attached to structure) that are the condominium corporation's responsibility. Finishes and owner-installed fixtures that are unit owner property are typically not within the corporation's inspection scope — but the line in practice requires review of the specific condominium's declaration and description documents.

The January 1, 2025 deadline for pre-2001 buildings was the critical near-term milestone. Building owners or condominium corporations that missed this deadline are in non-compliance with the regulation — municipalities have authority to require immediate compliance and to enter and inspect premises, with associated penalties under the Building Code Act.

Who Is a Qualified Inspector Under O. Reg. 59/20

The regulation defines a "qualified person" for purposes of the balcony inspection as:

  • A Professional Engineer (P.Eng.) licensed in Ontario by Professional Engineers Ontario (PEO), or
  • A person who performs the inspection under the direct supervision of a P.Eng. licensed in Ontario

In practice, this means the inspection report must be reviewed and sealed (stamped) by a licensed Ontario P.Eng. The individual who performs the physical field inspection may be a field technician, engineering intern, or junior engineer — but the professional accountability, the report seal, and the condition classifications are the P.Eng.'s responsibility.

Licensed home inspectors, general contractors, and building envelope consultants without P.Eng. status do not qualify under this regulation, and their reports do not satisfy the legal requirement.

What the Inspection Covers

A thorough O. Reg. 59/20 inspection by a structural P.Eng. includes:

  • Balcony slab: Visual inspection and sounding (hammer tapping) for concrete delamination, spalling, cracking, carbonation, and chloride-induced corrosion of embedded reinforcing steel
  • Soffit / underside: Inspection from below for cracking, rust staining (indicative of reinforcing corrosion), spalling, and water infiltration evidence
  • Railings and guards: Inspection of structural condition, post anchorage to the slab, glass panel condition (if glazed), and compliance with current Ontario Building Code guard height requirements (min. 1,070 mm for residential balconies above 600 mm from grade)
  • Drainage: Confirmation that drains are clear and sloped to drain; evidence of ponding water that accelerates concrete deterioration
  • Waterproofing: Visual inspection of traffic membrane, coating, or topping slab condition where visible; inspection of curb details and penetrations
  • Connection to building: Assessment of the structural connection of the balcony slab to the building frame, including any visible ledger plate, bearing condition, or expansion joint details
  • Post-tensioning: Where visible post-tensioning anchors or dead-end pockets are present, inspection for corrosion and tendon failure indicators

The Inspection Report: Required Contents

O. Reg. 59/20 specifies minimum contents for the inspection report. A compliant report must include:

  • Building address and legal description
  • Identification of each inspected structure (balcony, terrace, walkway, or exit) — typically by unit number or floor/directional designation
  • Date of inspection
  • Description of observed conditions for each structure, including photographs
  • Condition classification for each structure (Good Repair / Requires Repair / Immediate Hazard)
  • Description of recommended remedial actions for any structures not in Good Repair
  • Description of any conditions that require immediate access restriction
  • Signature and seal of the qualified engineer (P.Eng.)

Reports must be retained by the owner for at least 10 years and must be provided to the municipality upon request. For condominium buildings, the report is also typically required to be shared with the condominium board and should inform the Reserve Fund Study.

Response to Unsafe Conditions

If the P.Eng. classifies any structure as an Immediate Hazard, the owner must:

  1. Immediately restrict access to the affected balcony, terrace, or walkway (barricade installation, door locks, notification to occupants)
  2. Notify the relevant municipality (building department) of the unsafe condition without delay
  3. Begin emergency repair measures to address the condition as soon as practical
  4. Obtain a building permit for structural repairs if the scope requires one under the Ontario Building Code
  5. Have repairs reviewed and documented by the P.Eng. once complete

Repair Requirements and Timelines

For conditions classified as Requires Repair:

  • All required repairs must be completed within 12 months of the inspection report date
  • Repairs must restore the structure to a condition that satisfies the Ontario Building Code requirements for structural safety and good repair
  • Structural repairs (concrete spall repair, railing replacement, post-tensioning tendon replacement) require a building permit from the local municipality under OBC Section 8
  • Completed repairs should be documented and the P.Eng. should provide a completion report confirming the repairs were performed as recommended

Ontario Building Code Requirements for Balcony Repairs

When structural repairs are performed, they must comply with the current Ontario Building Code (OBC), latest edition. Key OBC requirements for balconies include:

  • Structural loads: Balconies must be designed for a minimum 4.8 kPa live load (OBC Div. B, Article 4.1.5.3)
  • Guards (railings): All balconies more than 600 mm above grade require guards at least 1,070 mm high for residential use, at least 1,500 mm for suites above the tenth storey
  • Glass guards: When glass panels are used in guards, they must be designed and installed in accordance with OBC Division B Section 9.6. Tempered or laminated tempered glass is required.
  • Concrete repair: Concrete spall repairs must restore structural capacity and use materials compatible with the existing concrete. CSA A23.1/A23.2 repair procedures apply.

Condo Corporation Obligations Under the Condo Act 1998

Condominium corporations in Ontario have overlapping obligations under both O. Reg. 59/20 and the Condominium Act, 1998:

  • The Condo Act requires corporations to repair and maintain the common elements (which include balcony structural elements in most declarations)
  • The corporation's Reserve Fund Study (required under Condo Act s.94) must account for balcony repair and replacement as capital expenditure line items
  • The O. Reg. 59/20 inspection report feeds directly into the Reserve Fund Study and should be provided to the Reserve Fund Study engineer at each update cycle
  • Failure to maintain common elements exposes the condominium corporation to liability under both the Condo Act and common law negligence principles

Frequently Asked Questions

What is O. Reg. 59/20 and which Ontario buildings must comply?

O. Reg. 59/20 is Ontario's mandatory balcony and exterior structure inspection regulation under the Building Code Act, 1992. It applies to residential buildings 3 or more storeys in height that have exterior balconies, terraces, exterior walkways, or exterior exits. Both condominium corporations and rental apartment owners must comply. The first major deadline (for pre-2001 buildings) was January 1, 2025.

How often are Ontario balcony inspections required under O. Reg. 59/20?

Every 5 years from the date of the most recent qualified inspection. For pre-2001 buildings, the first inspection was required by January 1, 2025. New buildings must be inspected within 5 years of first occupancy.

Who is qualified to perform Ontario balcony inspections under O. Reg. 59/20?

A Professional Engineer (P.Eng.) licensed in Ontario, or a person working under the direct supervision of a P.Eng. The report must bear the P.Eng.'s signature and seal. Home inspectors, contractors, or building envelope consultants without P.Eng. status do not qualify.

What happens if a balcony is found unsafe in Ontario?

If classified as an Immediate Hazard: the owner must immediately restrict access, notify the municipality, and begin emergency repairs. If classified as Requires Repair: repairs must be completed within 12 months. All work must satisfy the Ontario Building Code and may require a building permit from the local municipality.

What does an Ontario balcony structural inspection include?

The P.Eng. inspects: balcony slab (visual + hammer sounding for delamination), soffit, railings and guard anchorage, drainage conditions, waterproofing, structural connection to the building, and post-tensioning elements. Photography, condition classification, and recommended remedial actions are required in the written report.

What is included in the O. Reg. 59/20 inspection report?

The report must include: building identification, listing of all inspected structures, inspection date, observed conditions with photographs, condition classification for each structure, recommended repairs, access restriction requirements, and the P.Eng.'s signature and seal. Reports must be retained for at least 10 years.
