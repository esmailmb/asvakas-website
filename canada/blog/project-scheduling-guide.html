Construction Project Scheduling for Ontario Structural Projects

Why Scheduling Matters on Structural Projects

Construction project scheduling is not a bureaucratic formality — it is the primary management tool for controlling cost, quality, and time on any non-trivial construction project. On structural projects specifically, the schedule drives: concrete pour sequencing (with implications for formwork reuse, crane time, and achieving design strength before loading); structural steel delivery and erection sequences (crane picks, connections, temporary bracing); permit review cycles that gate construction start; and construction administration coordination for shop drawing review, RFI responses, and field inspections.

Ontario's construction industry has increasingly recognized that schedule delay is one of the most common and expensive sources of project risk. Late completion triggers liquidated damages clauses, financing cost overruns (Ontario construction loans typically accrue interest at prime + 1–2%), loss of occupancy revenue, and costly acceleration measures. Proactive, technically rigorous scheduling from project inception is the primary mitigation.

Critical Path Method (CPM) Scheduling

Critical Path Method scheduling models the project as a network of activities with defined durations and logical relationships. Developed in the 1950s (DuPont and US Navy Polaris program), CPM remains the industry standard for construction planning because it explicitly identifies which activities control total project duration — the "critical path" — and which activities have schedule "float" (time buffer before they impact the critical path.)

Key CPM Concepts

  • Activity: A discrete unit of work with a defined duration, resource requirement, predecessor activities, and successor activities.
  • Logic link: The defined relationship between activities — Finish-to-Start (FS), Start-to-Start (SS), Finish-to-Finish (FF), with lag or lead time.
  • Critical path: The sequence of activities with zero float — the longest path through the network from start to completion.
  • Total float: The amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the project completion date. Activities with zero float are critical.
  • Free float: The amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the early start of any successor activity.

CPM vs. Gantt Charts

Gantt charts — bar charts showing activities and their durations against a calendar — are widely used for communicating schedules to clients and site teams. However, Gantt charts do not explicitly model logic dependencies — they show what is planned but not why. CPM networks provide the analytical foundation; Gantt charts provide the visual communication layer. Effective scheduling practice for Ontario ICI projects uses both: CPM analysis in Primavera P6 or MS Project, with Gantt-format reports for site teams and owner meetings.

What a Complete Construction Schedule Contains

A complete construction schedule for an Ontario ICI or residential project should contain:

  1. Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): A hierarchical decomposition of project scope — from project level down to work package, typically matching the contract's Schedule of Values.
  2. Activities with durations: All construction activities, submittals, procurement, inspections, and owner-furnishing activities. Duration estimates based on crew productivity rates, quantity takeoffs, and project-specific constraints.
  3. Logic links: All predecessor-successor relationships with defined link types and lags. Particularly important: structural concrete pour dependencies (curing time before formwork stripping per CSA A23.1), steel connection completion before roof loading, and inspections that must precede next phase.
  4. Calendars and constraints: Working calendars reflecting Ontario labour agreements (7-day/week or 5-day/week), statutory holidays, weather constraints for concrete placement (no placement below 5°C without approved cold weather plan), and contractual milestone constraints.
  5. Critical path identification: Visible identification of critical path activities so project management resources can be focused on risk mitigation.
  6. Milestone list: Key contractual milestones — permit issue, structural steel delivery, concrete topping, envelope completion, occupancy permit.
  7. Procurement and submittals register: Integrated list of long-lead items (structural steel, precast, elevator, curtain wall) with required order dates working back from required delivery dates.

Key Structural Milestones in Ontario Projects

Structural engineers actively contribute to schedule development by defining the technical constraints that govern key milestones:

  • Permit-ready drawings issue: The date by which sealed structural drawings must be submitted to the municipal building department. For Toronto Building, typical structural review takes 40–80 business days — this milestone gates construction start and must be scheduled backward from the planned construction start date.
  • Foundation completion: Governing milestone for superstructure start. Includes excavation, shoring installation, geotechnical inspection, foundation pour, and curing time before backfill loading.
  • Structural steel fabrication lead time: Ontario fabricators typically require 12–20 weeks from purchase order to delivery for standard W-section framing. Complex connections, large sections, or specialty steel can extend lead time to 24–36 weeks. The steel order must be placed before detailed connection design is complete (erection drawing approval cycle must be factored in).
  • Concrete deck pours: Cast-in-place slabs require curing time before subsequent floors are loaded. Per CSA A23.1, residential concrete typically achieves 70% of 28-day strength (the threshold for reshoring removal) in approximately 7–14 days depending on temperature. Heavily loaded transfer slabs may require 28-day strength confirmation before loading.
  • Construction administration submittals: Shop drawing review cycles must be incorporated into the schedule — typically 10–15 business days for structural steel, 5–10 business days for concrete reinforcing. Late submittal review responses from the engineer are a common source of contactor delay claims.

Ontario-Specific Scheduling Constraints

Cold Weather Concrete

Concrete placement in Ontario is constrained by cold weather from approximately November through March. CSA A23.1 prohibits placing concrete when the ambient temperature is below 5°C without an approved cold weather protection plan (heated enclosures, insulation, admixtures). Cold weather concrete adds cost and complexity — most Ontario projects plan to schedule concrete work for the October–November window at the latest and resume in April. Projects with critical schedule constraints may implement heated enclosure systems, but costs can add 10–25% to concrete costs during winter months.

Frozen Ground & Excavation

Excavation in Ontario frozen ground (typically December–March depending on depth) typically requires frost-breaking equipment (hydraulic breakers, frost blankets) and is significantly slower than unfrozen excavation. Foundation concrete cannot be placed on frozen subgrade — ground temperature must be confirmed above 0°C at bearing depth before pour.

Ontario Construction Act — Monthly Progress Draws

Ontario's Construction Act (2019 amendments) introduced prompt payment requirements: owners must pay the contract price (or disputed portions thereof) within 28 days of receiving a proper invoice. Construction schedules must align with the billing cycle — monthly progress measurements must reflect actual work-in-place quantities at the billing date to support accurate owner payment certification. Schedule slippage that leads to overbilling is a significant source of construction team disputes.

Delay Claims Under Ontario Construction Contracts

When construction projects fall behind schedule, delay claims arise. Under CCDC 2 (standard stipulated price contract used across Ontario ICI construction), delay entitlement and quantum are separate issues:

  • Entitlement: Was the delay caused by an owner-responsible event (design change, delayed permit, unforeseen site conditions) or a contractor-responsible event (labour shortage, equipment breakdown, subcontractor failure)?
  • Critical path impact: Did the delay event affect a critical path activity? Non-critical activities with float may be delayed without extending project completion — only delays to critical path activities create entitlement to time extensions.
  • Concurrent delays: Where both parties contributed to delay, most Ontario arbitration panels apply concurrent delay principles to apportion the delay impact. The contractor cannot recover cost for an owner-caused delay if the contractor was simultaneously causing an equal or greater delay through their own fault.
  • Forensic schedule analysis: Delay claims require retrospective CPM analysis — comparing the as-planned schedule to the as-built schedule using contemporaneous records (daily reports, progress photos, submittals logs, RFI records, delivery records). Frequently Asked Questions

What is a critical path method (CPM) schedule?

A CPM schedule models a project as a network of activities with defined durations and logical dependencies. The critical path is the longest sequence of dependent activities — delays on the critical path directly delay project completion. CPM is the industry standard for Ontario ICI construction scheduling.

Who prepares the construction schedule in Ontario?

Under CCDC 2 and similar Ontario contracts, the general contractor prepares and maintains the baseline and updated construction schedule. The structural engineer contributes structural milestone dates (permit issue, steel delivery lead times, concrete curing constraints) and coordinates CA review periods within the schedule.

How does winter affect structural construction in Ontario?

Ontario winters constrain concrete placement (prohibited below 5°C ambient without cold weather plan per CSA A23.1), slow excavation in frozen ground, and reduce labour productivity. Projects without heated enclosures plan to complete exposed concrete work by November and resume in April, adding 4–5 months to the effective concrete construction season.

What is a time extension claim?

A claim by the contractor for additional contract time (and often additional compensation) due to a delay event for which the owner bears risk under the contract — such as design changes, delayed permit approval, unforeseen site conditions, or force majeure. The claim must demonstrate that the delay was owner-caused and affected the critical path.

How long does it take to get a building permit in Toronto?

Toronto Building's service standard is 10–20 business days for simple Part 9 residential projects and 40–80 business days for Part 3 commercial buildings and complex alterations. Complete, accurate, sealed drawings submitted by qualified professionals significantly reduce review cycles. Factor Toronto's permit timeline into your project baseline schedule from day one.
