The OBC Framework for Existing Buildings

The Ontario Building Code (O.Reg 332/12 under the Building Code Act, S.O. 1992, c.23) is the primary regulatory framework governing how buildings are constructed and altered in Ontario. For existing buildings — those built before the current OBC took effect — a nuanced set of provisions determines when and how current code requirements must be applied.

The general principle is captured in OBC Sentence 1.3.1.2.(1): an existing building may remain in use subject to the requirements in force when it was constructed, provided it is maintained in a safe condition. However, whenever an owner undertakes alterations, additions, or changes of use requiring a building permit, new OBC requirements come into play.

OBC Part 11: Renovation Requirements

OBC Part 11 (Renovation) governs all alterations to existing buildings. Key provisions engineering professionals must understand:

The "No Regression" Principle

OBC Sentence 11.3.1.1.(1) requires that alterations must not result in a building that is less compliant with the OBC than it was before the work commenced. If a proposed alteration would make a currently-acceptable condition non-compliant — for example, reducing headroom below the existing (compliant) dimension — it is not permitted.

Additions Must Meet Current OBC

Any addition to an existing building — a new floor, an expanded floor plate, a new structural bay — must comply with the current OBC requirements applicable to the addition itself and to any affected portions of the existing building.

When Structural Upgrade Is Required

The OBC requires full structural upgrading of the affected floor area when an addition increases the total floor area by more than 30% or when a change of use increases the occupancy classification. Structural upgrades typically include bringing lateral load resisting systems (seismic and wind) up to current standards and verifying existing foundation capacity for the increased loads.

Change of Use Triggers

Ontario operators frequently convert buildings between occupancies — office to residential, industrial to mixed-use, retail to daycare. Each major group change in the OBC's occupancy classification system can trigger structural review requirements:

Change of UseStructural Trigger
Industrial (F) → Residential (C)Full structural review; floor loads likely adequate but verify; OBC seismic upgrade
Office → Post-Secondary Education (D→A)Live load increase from 2.4 kPa to 4.8 kPa may require floor strengthening
Office (D) → Storage (F-2)Load increase requires structural verification; floor-to-floor heights may need review
Any occupancy → Assembly (A-1 to A-4)Human-induced vibration check (NBCC); egress structural review

Balcony Inspection Requirements — Ontario Regulation 59/20

Enacted under the Building Code Act following concerns about balcony safety in Ontario's aging high-rise stock, O.Reg 59/20 established mandatory periodic balcony inspections for residential multi-unit buildings. Key requirements:

  • Who must comply: Owners of residential buildings of 5 or more storeys containing 10 or more units, constructed before 2001, that have one or more balconies
  • First inspection: Must be completed within 5 years of the building's construction (if not already done), or within 5 years of the regulation's coming into force
  • Inspection frequency: Every 5 years following the previous inspection
  • Who can inspect: A registered engineer (P.Eng.) or architect (OAA)
  • Filing: The inspection report must be filed with the municipality (e.g., Toronto Building)
  • Repairs: Required repairs identified by the inspection must be completed within a timeframe specified in the Building Code Act's regulations and as directed by the municipal building official
⚠️

Ontario's balcony inspection regime is ongoing and expanding. Building owners who have not complied with O.Reg 59/20 risk municipal orders, fines, and civil liability if balcony failures occur. Retain a P.Eng. to assess compliance status and establish an inspection schedule.

OHSA Structural Obligations

The Occupational Health and Safety Act creates structural obligations for property owners and employers. Key regulations:

  • OHSA R.R.O. 1990, Reg. 851 (Industrial Establishments): Section 45 requires that buildings and structures must be of sufficient strength and stability to support all loads and forces they are reasonably expected to be subjected to — employers have an ongoing duty to maintain structural integrity in workplaces
  • OHSA O.Reg 213/91 (Construction Projects): Governs all construction activity on the property — shoring, excavation, demolition, scaffold — with specific requirements for engineer-signed designs when excavation is deeper than 1.2 m
  • OHSA O.Reg 490/09 (Designated Substances): Pre-demolition surveys for designated substances (asbestos, silica) in existing buildings are required before any structural work that disturbs building materials

Property Standards & Municipal Orders

In addition to the provincial OBC and OHSA framework, Ontario municipalities enforce Property Standards By-laws under the Municipal Act, 2001. Toronto's Property Standards By-law No. 629-2009 sets minimum standards for building maintenance, including structural elements. A Property Standards Officer can issue orders requiring: repair or replacement of deteriorated structural elements; remediation of unsafe conditions; and demolition of unsafe structures that cannot be feasibly repaired.

When structural deficiencies are identified through inspections, engineering reports, or complaints, the municipality will issue Compliance Orders with specific repair timelines. Non-compliance can result in the municipality undertaking the repairs and recovering costs from the property owner — plus administrative penalties.

Toronto-Specific Requirements

Toronto imposes additional requirements beyond the provincial baseline:

  • eTender/OpenBid: All Toronto city procurement for repair work over threshold values is competitively tendered
  • Heritage buildings: Alterations to heritage buildings designated under Ontario Heritage Act Part IV require Heritage Impact Assessments and Heritage Conservation Plans approved by Heritage Preservation Services before building permit issuance
  • Ravine and Natural Features: Work within 10m of ravines requires site plan review and may require structural certifications regarding slope stability
  • Tree Protection: Excavation within the Critical Root Zone of protected trees requires arborist and structural engineer coordination

Frequently Asked Questions

What does OBC Part 11 require for existing buildings?

Part 11 governs renovations and alterations. Key rules: alterations must not make the building less OBC-compliant than before; additions must meet current OBC; and change of use to a higher occupancy classification may trigger full structural upgrade of affected areas.

What is Ontario's balcony inspection requirement?

O.Reg 59/20 requires owners of residential buildings with 5+ storeys, 10+ units, built before 2001 to have balconies inspected by a P.Eng. or OAA architect every 5 years, with reports filed with the municipality. Non-compliance risks orders and civil liability.

What OHSA obligations apply to existing Ontario buildings?

OHSA Reg 851 requires ongoing structural integrity in industrial establishments. OHSA Reg 213/91 governs construction activity. Designated substance surveys are required before structural work disturbing building materials. MLITSD enforces all OHSA obligations.

What must an owner do if Toronto Building finds structural deficiencies?

Retain a P.Eng. immediately to assess the deficiency and prepare a remediation plan. File required building permit applications. Complete repairs within the timeline specified in the municipal order. Failure to comply enables the city to undertake repairs at the owner's expense plus penalties.
