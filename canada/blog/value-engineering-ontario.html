Value Engineering in Ontario Construction: Structural VE, CCDC 2 Contracts & Cost Reduction That Works

Value engineering on Ontario construction projects can produce meaningful structural cost reductions — 5–15% of the structural contract value on larger projects — when conducted as a disciplined process with qualified engineering involvement. But VE proposals that compromise structural performance, code compliance, or long-term durability are not true VE: they are scope cuts that create liability. This guide explains the Ontario VE process, how CCDC 2 contracts handle VE savings sharing, common structural VE items, and how structural engineers evaluate proposals responsibly.

What Value Engineering Is (and Is Not)

Value engineering, developed from Lawrence Miles' work in industrial manufacturing, applies a function-focused analysis to construction: for each building system and component, ask what function it performs and whether that function can be achieved at lower cost with equivalent or better performance. Applied to structural engineering in Ontario:

  • True VE: Substituting a two-way post-tensioned slab for a conventional flat plate at the same floor-to-floor height, achieving 20% concrete volume reduction, savings of $50/m² on a 10,000 m² floor plate, while maintaining equivalent structural performance, code compliance, and constructability
  • Not VE (scope or quality reduction): Reducing slab thickness below what the structural engineer designed, accepting lower compressive strength concrete, omitting reinforcement specified for durability, or reducing the number of piles specified by the geotechnical engineer

The distinction matters professionally: a structural engineer who accepts a "VE" proposal that is actually a quality reduction takes on liability for the change. True VE proposals require the structural engineer to re-design for the new system and confirm OBC compliance.

VE Under CCDC 2 in Ontario

CCDC 2 (Stipulated Price Contract) is the most common construction contract form for Ontario building projects. The standard CCDC 2 terms do not inherently include a VE savings-sharing mechanism — this must be added via supplementary conditions. Typical Ontario VE supplementary conditions include:

  • VE proposal period: Contractor may submit VE proposals within a defined period after award (typically 30–90 days for post-award VE)
  • Contractor bears redesign cost: The contractor is responsible for the cost of any engineering redesign required to implement the VE change
  • Engineer's review timeline: The structural engineer of record is given a defined review period (typically 15–30 days) to evaluate structural VE proposals
  • Owner's right to reject: Owner may reject any VE proposal for any reason, including scheduling impact, architectural impact, or professional engineering concerns
  • Savings sharing: Net verifiable savings (reduced contract price less redesign cost and schedule impact cost) are shared — typically 50/50 or 60/40 (owner/contractor)
CCDC 5A — Construction Management: On CM At-Risk projects using CCDC 5A, VE is typically integrated into preconstruction services as part of the CM's advisory role. The CM proposes and analyses alternate structural systems during design development, before the construction documents are complete — when the cost and schedule impact of addressing VE suggestions is lowest.

The VE Workshop Process

On complex Ontario projects, formal VE workshops follow a defined methodology:

  1. Information collection: Gather the structural drawings, specifications, cost estimate, environmental reports, and schedule to understand the project baseline
  2. Function analysis: Break the structural system into its functional components — what does each element do, and what is its current cost per unit of function?
  3. Creative phase: Generate candidate alternatives for each function — no criticism, high quantity of ideas
  4. Evaluation phase: Screen ideas for feasibility — structural compliance, schedule impact, architectural integration, constructability, long-term performance
  5. Development phase: Develop the promising alternatives in sufficient detail to quantify savings and identify necessary engineering work
  6. Presentation: Present accepted VE proposals to the owner and design team with estimated savings, risk assessment, and implementation path

How the Structural Engineer Evaluates VE

The structural engineer of record evaluates VE proposals against multiple criteria before accepting or rejecting:

  • Code compliance: Does the proposed system meet OBC Part 4, NBCC 2020 gravity, wind, and seismic load requirements? Does it meet CSA material and design standards?
  • Gravity and lateral performance: Are deflections, vibrations, and strength adequate under the new system?
  • Detailing and constructability: Can the alternate system be detailed and built to achieve its design performance, or are there critical constructability issues?
  • Interface impacts: How does the change affect ceiling heights (mechanical clearances), floor-to-floor height (total building height, zoning compliance), column layout (parking efficiency, unit planning), curtain wall attachment, or phasing?
  • Long-term performance and durability: Will the alternate provide equivalent service life? Thin post-tensioned slabs may have higher long-term corrosion risk than thicker conventional slabs in certain environments.

VE Risks and How to Mitigate Them

The primary risks associated with contractor-initiated structural VE in Ontario:

  • Schedule risk: Major structural system changes require redesign time — if the VE proposal comes after the building permit is issued, the schedule impact of a permit revision may eliminate any cost savings
  • Design liability shift: When the structural engineer of record re-designs for a VE change, they assume professional responsibility for the revised design. Ensure the SER's engagement terms address VE change redesign scope and additional fees
  • Coordination gaps: A structural VE change that affects slab thickness or floor-to-floor height must be coordinated with mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and architectural design — coordination failure can create costly conflicts revealed only during construction

Value Engineering in Preconstruction

The highest-value VE opportunity is structural system selection during early design (schematic design or design development) — before construction documents are complete and before the building permit is filed. At this stage, alternate structural systems can be fully costed and optimized without permit revision costs or contractor schedule pressure. Ontario owners planning complex or large-scale construction projects should engage the structural engineer to evaluate alternative structural systems at the conceptual design stage, including parametric cost comparisons of slab systems and foundation types in the Ontario market before committing to a structural system.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is value engineering in Ontario construction?

Value engineering (VE) is a systematic approach to identifying construction alternatives that achieve the same performance at lower cost. In structural engineering, this includes alternate slab systems, foundation types, connection methods, or material grades. True VE maintains code compliance, structural performance, and long-term durability — it is not scope reduction or quality reduction, which creates liability. The structural engineer of record must evaluate and stamp any revised design implementing a VE change.

How does VE work under CCDC 2 in Ontario?

CCDC 2 doesn't include VE provisions natively — they must be added via supplementary conditions. Typical provisions allow the contractor to submit VE proposals within a defined period after award, require the contractor to bear redesign costs, give the structural engineer a review period (15–30 days), and share net savings (typically 50/50) between owner and contractor. The owner retains the right to reject any VE proposal. Major structural VE changes after permit issuance should be carefully evaluated for schedule impact, which can eliminate the financial benefit.

What are common structural VE items in Ontario?

Common Ontario structural VE items include: switching from conventional flat plate to post-tensioned slabs ($40–80/m² saving); changing from driven steel piles to augered cast-in-place piles; simplifying steel moment connections to shear tab connections where the lateral system allows; optimizing concrete strength grades; and substituting smaller column grids that eliminate transfer beams. Foundation alternates require geotechnical and structural engineer coordination and are most feasible when soil conditions are confirmed before the VE decision.

Who evaluates VE proposals in Ontario?

The structural engineer of record evaluates structural VE proposals for code compliance, structural performance, constructability, interface impacts with other systems (mechanical clearances, floor heights, curtain wall), and long-term durability. The SER must be willing to stamp the revised design implementing the VE change — if the SER determines the alternate system is unsatisfactory for any technical reason, the VE proposal should be rejected regardless of the cost savings. VE evaluation should include additional fees for the SER's engineering review and redesign work.
