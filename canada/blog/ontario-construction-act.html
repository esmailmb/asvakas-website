Ontario Construction Act 2018: Prompt Payment, Construction Liens, ODACC Adjudication & Engineer Rights

Ontario's Construction Act 2018 (Bill 142) fundamentally reformed how construction payment, disputes, and lien rights work in the province. The amendments — effective for most contracts from October 1, 2019 — introduced mandatory prompt payment (the 28-day cycle), ODACC adjudication for fast binding dispute resolution, and modernized construction lien law. For structural engineers, the Act provides powerful tools to collect unpaid fees: lien rights against the improved property, prompt payment remedies, and ODACC adjudication without going to court. This guide explains the key provisions every Ontario contractor, owner, and engineer should know.

Bill 142 Overview — What Changed

The Construction Act 2018 (S.O. 2017, c. 24 — formerly Construction Lien Act, R.S.O. 1990) made the most significant reforms to Ontario construction law in decades:

  • Prompt payment: Mandatory payment timelines throughout the payment chain — owner to contractor (28 days), contractor to subcontractor (7 days after receiving owner payment), subcontractor down (7 days)
  • ODACC adjudication: A new mandatory adjudication mechanism for payment disputes, providing binding interim decisions within 30 days — without going to court
  • Modernized lien law: Updated lien timelines, new rules for public sector contracts, clarified holdback provisions, and improved trust fund protections
  • Substantial performance: Clarified definition and publication requirements to provide more certainty about when holdback can be released

The effective date is contracts for improvements entered into on or after October 1, 2019. Contracts for public sector projects using the capital investment planning approvals process use phased-in dates for certain provisions.

Prompt Payment: The 28-Day Cycle

The prompt payment regime creates a strict cascade:

PayerMust Pay WithinTrigger
Owner → Contractor28 daysReceipt of proper invoice
Contractor → Subcontractor7 daysReceipt of owner's payment
Subcontractor → Sub-subcontractor7 daysReceipt of contractor's payment

A "proper invoice" is a written invoice that includes: the contractor's name and address, the date, information identifying the contract, a description of the services/materials, the amount claimed, the period covered, and any other information prescribed by the contract. An improper invoice is not a proper invoice — the payer can reject it, and the payment clock does not start until a compliant invoice is received.

Notice of Non-Payment

A paying party who disputes all or part of an invoice must serve a written notice of non-payment within specified deadlines:

  • Owner → Contractor: Notice of non-payment must be served within 14 days of receiving the contractor's proper invoice (leaving 14 more days for payment of the undisputed amount)
  • Contractor → Subcontractor: Notice of non-payment must be served within 7 days of the deadline to pay the subcontractor

If a paying party fails to serve a notice of non-payment on time, the full invoiced amount becomes due — even if there are legitimate disputes about some of the claimed amounts. This makes timely issuance of notices of non-payment a critical obligation for owners and contractors under the 2018 Act.

Missed Deadline = Full Payment Due: Ontario courts have confirmed that failure to serve a notice of non-payment within the prescribed period means the payer has waived the right to dispute the invoice amount under the prompt payment regime — the full amount becomes payable by the deadline. Owners and contractors maintaining tight project controls are critical to avoid inadvertent obligations.

10% Statutory Holdback

The Construction Act requires that at each stage of the payment chain, 10% of amounts earned be retained as a statutory holdback:

  • Owner retains 10% from each contractor payment
  • Contractor retains 10% from each subcontractor payment
  • Subcontractor retains 10% from payments to sub-subcontractors

The holdback creates a lien fund — lien claimants can assert claims against the holdback. The holdback cannot be released until the lien period expires and all liens are cleared. For contracts with multiple stages or certifiable phases, a separate holdback period may apply to each phase.

Post-substantial performance, the holdback release process is triggered by publication of the Certificate of Substantial Performance — then the 45-day lien registration period runs, after which the holdback can be released if no liens are registered (or all liens are cleared).

Construction Liens in Ontario

The construction lien is the fundamental payment protection tool under the Construction Act. Any person who supplies services or materials to an improvement has lien rights against the owner's interest in the premises. Persons entitled to a lien include:

  • General contractors
  • Subcontractors and sub-subcontractors at any tier
  • Material suppliers
  • Equipment lessors
  • Engineers, architects, and surveyors who provide services in relation to the improvement
  • Workers

The lien amount is the unpaid amount owed to the lienor for services or materials supplied. The lien creates a charge against the title of the improved land — effectively encumbering the property until the lien is cleared (paid, vacated by security, or discharged by court order).

Lien Registration Timelines

Critical lien timelines under the Construction Act:

  • Lien must be preserved within 60 days of the last date on which the lienor supplied services or materials to the premises
  • After registration, lien must be perfected within 90 days by commencing an action in Ontario Superior Court and registering a Certificate of Action on title
  • Failure to preserve or perfect a lien within these deadlines results in the lien expiring — the deadlines are absolute, although courts have narrow discretion to extend time in extraordinary circumstances

For structural engineers, the "last day of supplying services" is typically the date of the last professional service rendered — the last field review visit, the last design letter, or the last General Review field review. Engineers should track their last date of service carefully and calendar the 60-day lien deadline if payment issues arise.

ODACC Adjudication

ODACC (Ontario Dispute Adjudication for Construction Contracts) provides a mandatory adjudication pathway for payment disputes that is faster and cheaper than court:

  • Any party can trigger adjudication at any time after a dispute arises, by serving a Notice of Adjudication on the other party
  • The parties agree on an adjudicator (from ODACC's roster) within 4 days; if they cannot agree, ODACC appoints one
  • The adjudicator makes a decision within 30 days of the adjudicator's receipt of notice — extendable by consent
  • The decision is binding and immediately enforceable as a court judgment — the losing party must pay promptly
  • Either party may bring the same issue to court or arbitration after adjudication for a final determination (adjudication is "interim binding")
  • Adjudicator fees are paid by the losing party (unless the adjudicator orders otherwise)

Engineering Firms and the Construction Act

Structural engineering firms providing services on Ontario construction projects have important rights and obligations under the Construction Act:

  • Lien rights: Engineering services provided in relation to an improvement — design, review, inspection, General Review — qualify for lien rights. If an engineering firm is unpaid for these services, it can register a construction lien within 60 days of its last date of service.
  • Prompt payment eligibility: Engineering contracts structured as contracts for services in relation to an improvement may be subject to prompt payment if they fall within the Construction Act's definition of contract. Firms should review their engagement letters to assess eligibility and to ensure their invoicing is in the form of a "proper invoice."
  • ODACC adjudication for unpaid fees: If prompt payment timelines are not met, an engineering firm may trigger ODACC adjudication for a fast binding determination without going to court.
  • Trust fund obligations if acting as holding party: Where an engineering firm receives payments from an owner and is responsible for further distribution, trust fund provisions may apply.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the prompt payment requirement in Ontario's Construction Act?

Owners must pay contractors within 28 days of a proper invoice. Contractors must pay subcontractors within 7 days of receiving owner payment. Subcontractors must pay down the chain within 7 more days. If a paying party disputes an invoice, they must serve a written notice of non-payment within prescribed deadlines — typically 14 days for owners and 7 days for contractors — or the full invoiced amount becomes due. These rules apply to contracts entered after October 1, 2019.

How does a structural engineer register a construction lien in Ontario?

Engineers who provide services in relation to an Ontario construction improvement have lien rights. To preserve a lien, register a claim for lien with the Land Registry Office within 60 days of the last date services were supplied. The registration requires: lienor's identity, owner's identity, premises description, amount claimed, and last date of service — sworn. Then, within 90 days of registration, commence a court action and register a Certificate of Action to perfect the lien. Missing either deadline extinguishes the lien permanently.

What holdback percentage is required in Ontario?

10% statutory holdback is required at each level of the payment chain — owner retains 10% from contractor payments, contractor retains 10% from subcontractor payments. The holdback cannot be released until after substantial performance is published, the 45-day lien registration period expires, and all registered liens are cleared. The holdback serves as a fund against which lien claims can be made, protecting trade contractors and suppliers throughout the construction process.

What disputes can be resolved through ODACC adjudication?

ODACC adjudication can resolve disputes about: amounts payable under invoices; payment certificate valuation; payment or non-payment disputes; holdback amounts; notices of non-payment; and set-offs. Either party can trigger adjudication at any time by serving a Notice of Adjudication. An adjudicator makes a binding interim decision within 30 days — immediately enforceable as a court judgment. Structural engineers with unpaid fees can use ODACC to obtain fast binding payment direction without going through court proceedings.
