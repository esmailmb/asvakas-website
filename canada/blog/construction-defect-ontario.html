Construction Defect Claims in Ontario: Construction Act, ODACC Adjudication & Structural Engineering Evidence

Construction defects in Ontario buildings generate complex legal disputes involving the Ontario Construction Act 2018, Tarion warranty protections for new homes, ODACC adjudication for payment and certification disputes, and limitation periods that can bar claims if not acted on promptly. Structural engineers play a central role as technical investigators documenting defects and as expert witnesses in court. This guide explains the Ontario construction defect legal framework, limitation periods, ODACC adjudication, and Rule 53.03 expert witness obligations for structural engineers.

Types of Construction Defects in Ontario

Construction defects fall into four categories relevant to structural engineering involvement:

  • Design defects: Errors or omissions in the structural engineer's or architect's design — under-designed members, inadequate connection details, failure to account for code-required loads. These create professional liability exposure for the design professional.
  • Materials defects: Sub-standard, defective, or incorrectly specified materials — out-of-spec concrete (low strength), degraded rebar, improper lumber grade, defective steel. May be a contractor's responsibility, supplier's responsibility, or a materials testing deficiency.
  • Construction (workmanship) defects: Failure to build in conformance with design documents — incorrect member sizing, missing reinforcement, deficient connections, inadequate weld quality, improper grouting or fire-stopping. Most common structural defect category in new construction.
  • Subsurface/geotechnical defects: Foundation settlement, slope instability, or soil movement resulting from inadequate geotechnical investigation or failure to follow geotechnical recommendations.

Ontario Construction Act 2018 Overview

Ontario's Construction Act (formerly the Construction Lien Act), substantially amended by Bill 142 in 2017 and effective for most contracts from October 2019, governs the rights of parties on Ontario construction projects including:

  • Definition of substantial performance: The Construction Act defines when a contract is substantially performed — triggering holdback release deadlines and the start of lien periods. Dispute about whether substantial performance has been achieved often arises in defect situations where the owner argues defects prevent substantial performance.
  • Lien rights for contractors: Contractors, subcontractors, and suppliers have lien rights against the improved property for unpaid amounts under the Construction Act. A structural engineer who believes they have a defect-related claim against the contractor can register a claim for lien if unpaid.
  • Holdback requirements: Owners are required to retain a 10% statutory holdback from each payment to contractors. Holdback cannot be released while there are unsatisfied liens or during a defined period after substantial performance notice. The holdback serves as a fund against which defect-related claims can be made.

Tarion Warranty for New Homes

Tarion administers the Ontario New Home Warranties Plan Act warranty for new homes built by registered builders. Structural warranty coverage under Tarion:

Warranty PeriodCoverage
1 yearWorkmanship and materials defects (including visible structural issues)
2 yearsDefects to distribution systems and structural defects caused by contractor work
7 yearsMajor structural defects — defects in load-bearing portions resulting in collapse or imminent collapse risk

The seven-year major structural defect warranty is the most significant structural protection. To trigger the warranty, the defect must: be in a load-bearing element; result in actual structural collapse or pose an imminent threat of collapse; and be caused by the builder's work (not owner alterations or deferred maintenance). A P.Eng structural assessment is the key evidence in establishing a Tarion major structural defect claim.

ODACC Adjudication

The Ontario Dispute Adjudication for Construction Contracts (ODACC) authority was established under the Construction Act 2018 to provide a fast, binding, interim dispute resolution process for Ontario construction contracts. Key features:

  • Scope: Adjudication applies to disputes about payment under an invoice or certificate, amounts retained as holdback, payment or non-payment under a proposed payment certificate, set-offs, and notices of non-payment
  • Not within ODACC scope: Complex defect liability claims involving design negligence, structural failure causation analysis, or substantial performance disputes that require extensive expert evidence generally exceed ODACC scope and proceed through the courts
  • Process: Either party can trigger adjudication by serving a Notice of Adjudication. The parties agree on (or ODACC appoints) an adjudicator. The adjudicator makes a decision within 30 days of receiving a notice of adjudication response
  • Binding interim decision: The adjudicator's decision is binding and immediately enforceable but is "interim" — parties can still pursue the same issues in litigation or arbitration after adjudication

Limitation Periods

Ontario construction defect claims are subject to the Limitations Act, 2002:

  • Basic limitation period: 2 years from the date of discovery (when the claimant knew or reasonably ought to have known about the claim). For latent structural defects not visible at construction, the 2-year period begins when the defect becomes apparent or when a reasonable person in the claimant's position would have discovered it through reasonable diligence.
  • Ultimate limitation period: 15 years from the date the act or omission occurred (typically the date of construction). This is an absolute cut-off regardless of discovery — no matter how hidden the defect was, claims brought more than 15 years after the negligent construction act are statute-barred.
Latent Defect Timing: A structural defect that was not apparent at construction but causes visible damage 12 years later still has a viable claim — the 2-year period runs from discovery, and 12 + 2 = 14 years is within the 15-year ultimate. But wait until year 14 of the manifestation and the ultimate period may bar the claim. Engage a structural engineer as soon as a defect is suspected.

Structural Engineer as Expert Witness

In Ontario construction defect litigation, the structural engineer provides expert evidence on:

  • Whether the construction work conformed to the contract documents, the applicable codes (OBC, NBCC), and accepted engineering practice in Ontario
  • Whether an identified defect constitutes a "major structural defect" under the Tarion warranty definition
  • The cause and origin of structural failure or damage
  • The scope and cost of structural remediation required
  • Whether the design-of-record was adequate or whether the defect was attributable to a design error

Structural engineers serving as expert witnesses in Ontario must satisfy Rule 53.03 regarding independence, qualifications, and opinion disclosure — see below.

Rule 53.03 Obligations

Ontario Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 53.03 prescribes strict requirements for expert evidence in Ontario Superior Court proceedings. A structural engineer retained as a testifying expert must:

  1. Provide a signed expert report meeting the requirements of Form 53A — including: the expert's qualifications; the instructions received; the nature of the opinion and the reasons for it; the literature relied upon; and a signed acknowledgement of the expert's duty to the court
  2. Include the statement: "I acknowledge that it is my duty to provide evidence in relation to this proceeding that is fair, objective and non-partisan"
  3. Disclose the expert's relationship to the retaining parties
  4. Certify that the report contains all information the expert is willing to provide regarding the subject-matter of the evidence
  5. Serve the report at least 60 days before trial (90 days for the party with the burden of proof)

Non-compliant expert reports can be excluded from evidence — a significant tactical disadvantage in litigation. Structural engineers acting as testifying experts in Ontario must understand and satisfy these obligations before their engagement commences.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the limitation period for a construction defect claim in Ontario?

Ontario construction defect claims face a 2-year basic limitation period (from discovery) and a 15-year ultimate limitation period (from the act or omission). For latent defects not visible at construction, the 2-year period runs from when the defect was or should have been discovered. The 15-year ultimate period is an absolute bar regardless of discovery date. Engage a structural engineer as soon as a potential defect is identified — delay can result in a limitation bar even for clearly negligent construction work.

What is ODACC adjudication in Ontario construction?

ODACC (Ontario Dispute Adjudication for Construction Contracts) is a fast intermediate dispute resolution mechanism under the Construction Act 2018, primarily for payment disputes. Adjudicators make binding interim decisions within 30 days. Complex structural defect liability claims involving negligence causation and expert evidence generally exceed ODACC scope and must proceed through Ontario Superior Court or contractual arbitration. ODACC is not a substitute for litigation or arbitration in major structural defect disputes.

What structural defects does the Tarion seven-year warranty cover?

Tarion's seven-year major structural defect warranty covers defects in load-bearing portions of a new Ontario home that result in actual structural collapse or pose an imminent threat of collapse — caused by the builder's workmanship or materials. The defect must be in a structural element (foundation, loadbearing framing, roof structure) and must threaten structural integrity, not just cosmetic or habitability issues. A P.Eng structural assessment documenting the defect, its structural significance, and its cause is the key evidence for a Tarion seven-year claim.

What does Rule 53.03 require of a structural engineer expert witness in Ontario?

Rule 53.03 requires that an expert's signed report include: the expert's qualifications; instructions received; the opinion and its reasons; literature relied on; an acknowledgement of duty to the court (not to the retaining party); and a certification that the report is complete. Reports must be served 60–90 days before trial. Non-compliant expert reports can be excluded from evidence. Structural engineers should discuss Rule 53.03 requirements with the retaining lawyer at the outset of any expert witness engagement.
