CSA Standards in Ontario Structural Engineering: A23.3, S16, O86 & S6 Explained

Every structural engineer in Ontario designs to CSA standards referenced by the Ontario Building Code — but what do these standards actually contain, how do they relate to each other, and why do they matter to property owners, developers, and contractors? This guide breaks down the key CSA structural standards used in Ontario practice.

What Are CSA Standards?

CSA Group (formerly the Canadian Standards Association) is a not-for-profit organization that develops and publishes technical standards used across Canada. In the structural engineering domain, CSA standards are the mandatory design codes for each structural material system. They define how engineers calculate loads, size structural members, detail connections, and demonstrate compliance with safety requirements.

CSA standards are developed through a consensus process involving engineers, builders, fabricators, researchers, and regulators. They are updated typically every five years to incorporate new research, address field experience, and align with advances in materials science and construction practice. Once referenced by the OBC or NBCC, compliance with the relevant CSA standard is legally required for all structural design in Ontario.

How CSA Standards Relate to the OBC and NBCC

The relationship is a hierarchy:

  • NBCC 2020 (National Building Code of Canada) — sets structural loads (dead, live, snow, wind, seismic) in Part 4 and references specific CSA standards for structural design
  • OBC (O.Reg 332/12) — Ontario's provincial adoption of the NBCC with provincial amendments. For structural design (Part 4), the OBC adopts the NBCC's load provisions and CSA standard references without significant changes
  • CSA A23.3, S16, O86, S6 — the material-specific design standards referenced by Part 4 of the NBCC/OBC. Compliance with these standards is how engineers demonstrate that a structural design satisfies OBC Part 4 requirements
💡

Important: The OBC references specific editions of CSA standards. When a new CSA standard edition is published (e.g., A23.3:24 in 2024), it does not automatically become mandatory in Ontario until the next OBC amendment cycle adopts it. Engineers must design to the edition referenced by the currently-adopted OBC, unless the new edition is adopted by amendment or the project authority (some municipalities) requires it.

CSA A23.3 — Design of Concrete Structures

CSA A23.3 Design of Concrete Structures (current edition: A23.3:19, 2019) governs the structural design of all reinforced concrete, prestressed concrete, and post-tensioned concrete building elements in Canada. It is the Canadian equivalent of the American ACI 318.

Key Design Provisions

  • Strength design (limit states design): A23.3 uses factored resistance ≥ factored load effect (LRFD approach), consistent with NBCC load factors
  • Beams and slabs: Flexural design (balanced failure assumption, rebar limits), shear design (concrete contribution + stirrups), deflection control (span-to-depth ratios)
  • Columns: Combined axial-bending interaction, minimum eccentricity, confinement reinforcement requirements
  • Shear walls: Coupled shear walls, squat vs. flexure-dominated walls, seismic ductility — ductile, moderately ductile, and conventional construction classifications
  • Post-tensioned concrete: Bonded vs. unbonded tendons, anchorage zone design, secondary effects (P-delta, thermal)
  • Anchorage (Annex D): Anchor bolt and post-installed anchor design — tension, shear, and combined loading failure modes
  • Seismic detailing: Ductile moment-resisting frames (DMRF), ductile shear walls (DSW), capacity design principles for seismic regions

For Ontario, seismic design is significant for Ottawa and eastern Ontario (moderate to high seismic demand) and must be considered for all Part 4 structures even in Toronto (low to moderate). The A23.3 seismic provisions align with NBCC seismic hazard maps and site classification tables.

CSA S16 — Design of Steel Structures

CSA S16 Design of Steel Structures (current edition: S16:19, 2019) governs the structural design of steel-frame buildings and structures in Canada. It is the Canadian equivalent of AISC 360 and AISC 341.

Key Design Provisions

  • Limit states design: S16 uses ULS (ultimate limit states — strength) and SLS (serviceability limit states — deflection, vibration) design approaches
  • Member design: Beams (flexure, shear, lateral-torsional buckling), columns (axial compression, Euler buckling, slenderness limits), tension members (gross vs. net section), composite beams (shear stud design)
  • Connection design: Bolted connections (bearing-type and slip-critical), welded connections (AWS D1.1 as supplemented), base plates, gusset plates, moment connections
  • Stability: Frame stability (effective length, notional loads), bracing requirements for compression members
  • Seismic provisions: Type D (ductile) and Type MD (moderately ductile) moment-resisting frames and braced frames; capacity design requirements for seismic regions; protected zone requirements
  • Special structures: Plate girders, trusses, hangers, crane girders

CSA O86 — Engineering Design in Wood

CSA O86 Engineering Design in Wood (current edition: O86:19, 2019) governs the structural design of wood-frame, glulam, mass timber (CLT — cross-laminated timber, nail-laminated timber), and structural panel systems used in buildings. It is the Canadian equivalent of the American AWC NDS.

Key Design Provisions

  • Sawn lumber: Bending, shear, tension, compression design using tabulated species-specific characteristic values with adjustment factors for load duration (KD), wet service (KS), temperature (KT), and system factor (KH)
  • Glulam: Glued-laminated timber beams and columns — layup combinations, stress grades, and design for combined bending and axial loading
  • Mass timber (CLT, LVL, LSL): Panel design for bending, shear, rolling shear, compression perpendicular to grain — added to O86 to support Ontario's expanded mass timber height allowances under NBCC 2020
  • Connections: Nails, screws, bolts, lag bolts, split rings, shear plates — with adjustment factors; cross-laminated timber connections using self-tapping screws and structural adhesives
  • Fire resistance: Char-based fire design for exposed mass timber — Ontario adopted up to 12-storey mass timber under NBCC 2020 (encapsulated mass timber construction, EMTC)

CSA S6 — Design of Highway Bridges

CSA S6 Canadian Highway Bridge Design Code (current edition: S6:19, 2019) governs the structural design of bridges and highway structures in Canada. Where Ontario projects involve bridges — including private bridges on development sites, pedestrian bridges, and culverts — the design must follow S6.

  • Loads: CSA S6 defines the Canadian Live Load truck model (CL-625 truck and lane load) for highway bridges — different from the American HL-93 load model in AASHTO LRFD
  • Material provisions: S6 references A23.3 for concrete bridge structures and S16 for steel bridges, with additional bridge-specific provisions for fatigue design and inspection
  • MTO jurisdiction: In Ontario, provincial bridges are regulated by the Ministry of Transportation Ontario (MTO). MTO has supplementary specifications to S6 (OPSS — Ontario Provincial Standard Specifications) that govern design, materials, and construction practices for MTO projects

Code Adoption Cycles: When Does Ontario Adopt New Editions?

CSA standards are updated on approximately 5-year cycles. The adoption into Ontario practice follows the OBC amendment cycle:

  • The NBCC is updated by NRC (currently NBCC 2020 is the current edition)
  • Ontario adopts the NBCC through the OBC. The current OBC references NBCC 2012 provisions in many areas, with progressive updates through Ontario Regulation amendments
  • When the OBC is amended to reference a new NBCC edition or new CSA standard edition, a transition period is typically provided during which either the old or new edition may be used

In practice, Ontario engineers should confirm with the applicable building department which OBC edition is currently adopted for the project — particularly for CSA seismic provisions, where the NBCC 2020 seismic hazard model differs significantly from prior editions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are CSA standards and why do they matter for Ontario structural engineering?

CSA Group publishes the national standards that govern structural design in Canada. The Ontario Building Code and NBCC reference CSA standards as the mandatory design method for each structural material — concrete (A23.3), steel (S16), wood (O86), and bridges (S6). Every Ontario P.Eng. must follow the applicable CSA standard referenced by the OBC for any building permit project.

What is CSA A23.3 and what does it govern?

CSA A23.3 Design of Concrete Structures (current edition: A23.3:19) governs reinforced concrete, prestressed concrete, and post-tensioned concrete structural elements in Canadian buildings. Key provisions include strength design (limit states), beam and slab sizing, column design, shear wall seismic detailing, and post-installed anchor design (Annex D). It is Canada's equivalent of the American ACI 318.

What is CSA S16 and what structural systems does it govern?

CSA S16 Design of Steel Structures (current edition: S16:19) governs structural steel building design in Canada — beam, column, and connection design for gravity and seismic loading. It combines the functions of AISC 360 and AISC 341 in a single standard, using Canadian load factors from the NBCC and CSA's ductility classification system for seismic frames.

What is CSA O86 and when is it used in Ontario?

CSA O86 Engineering Design in Wood (current edition: O86:19) governs structural lumber, glulam, and mass timber (CLT, LVL) design in Canadian buildings. In Ontario, it is used for Part 4 wood structures, mid-rise wood-frame buildings, and increasingly for mass timber projects following NBCC 2020's allowance of up to 12-storey mass timber construction (encapsulated mass timber construction — EMTC).

What is the difference between the NBCC and the OBC for structural design in Ontario?

The NBCC is the national model building code published by NRC that sets structural load provisions and references CSA standards. The OBC is Ontario's provincial adoption of the NBCC with Ontario-specific amendments. For structural Part 4 design, the OBC and NBCC are essentially equivalent — both reference the same CSA standards (A23.3, S16, O86, S6). Ontario's amendments primarily affect architectural, fire safety, and energy provisions.
