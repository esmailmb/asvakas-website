Why Cost Estimation Matters

An accurate structural cost estimate is the financial foundation of any Ontario construction project. It allows property owners to confirm feasibility before committing to detailed design, supports lender due diligence for construction financing (TD, BMO, and other Canadian lenders require third-party cost estimates for most construction loans), enables developers to optimize structural systems for cost-effectiveness, and gives general contractors a reliable basis for competitive tendering.

Structural costs typically represent 20–40% of overall construction cost for commercial buildings and 8–18% for residential projects — making structural system selection one of the highest-leverage cost optimization decisions available to the design team. An engineer who understands both structural performance and cost trade-offs adds direct financial value, not just technical value.

Types of Cost Estimates

Ontario and Canadian industry practice aligns with the AACE International (Association for the Advancement of Cost Engineering) estimate classification system:

ClassProject DefinitionAccuracy RangeTypical Use
Class 5 (Order of Magnitude)Concept / Pre-design-50% to +100%Feasibility screening
Class 4Schematic design-30% to +50%Early budgeting
Class 3Design development-20% to +30%Design development budget
Class 2Construction documents (partial)-15% to +20%Pre-tender estimate
Class 1Final construction documents-5% to +10%Bid estimate / owner's estimate

Understanding the estimate class is critical to interpreting accuracy. A Class 5 feasibility estimate that comes in at $8M is telling you the project could reasonably cost anywhere from $4M to $16M — it's a screening tool, not a budget. Confusing estimate classes leads to systematic underbudgeting, a pervasive problem on Ontario construction projects.

Quantity Takeoff Process

A quantity takeoff (QTO) is the measurement and calculation of all structural material quantities from the engineer's drawings — the essential input for any cost estimate at Class 3 or better. The typical structural QTO workflow:

  1. Concrete volumes: Slabs, beams, columns, walls, and foundations measured by pour type and strength class (e.g., 30MPa, 35MPa, 40MPa). Concrete volume drives reinforcing, forming, and placement costs as well as potential premium for specified concrete properties (self-consolidating, low-alkali, high SCM replacement).
  2. Reinforcing steel: Rebar quantity is typically expressed in kg/m³ of concrete (reinforcing ratio) or as absolute tonnage per element type. The structural drawings' rebar schedules and typical sections are measured manually or with takeoff software (BlueBeam Revu, PlanSwift, or OnCenter). Post-tensioned systems require strand area calculations in addition to mild reinforcing.
  3. Structural steel: All members are tabulated by section size and length, then converted to tonnage using CSA 300W (or equivalent) steel density. Connection plates, base plates, and anchor bolts are also quantified. Tonnage by fabrication complexity (simple vs. complex connections) is tracked separately.
  4. Formwork: Contact area in m² for all formed surfaces (slab soffits, beam sides, column forms, wall forms). Formwork is often the largest labour cost driver in concrete construction and is frequently underestimated on estimate-stage takeoffs.
  5. Shoring and temporary works: Quantities of steel shores, flying form systems, or custom shoring for transfer structures.

Structural Cost Drivers in Ontario

Structural System Selection

The structural system drives cost more than almost any other single engineering decision. In Ontario's current market (2025–2026), typical structural system costs per m² of gross floor area:

  • Wood frame (Part 9, residential <4 storeys): $75–$130/m² structural scope
  • Mass timber (CLT/Glulam, 4-12 storeys): $180–$280/m² structural scope
  • Concrete flat-plate (mid-rise residential): $140–$210/m²
  • Concrete shear wall + flat-plate tower: $160–$240/m²
  • Steel frame (commercial/industrial): $120–$200/m²
  • Post-tensioned concrete (parking): $100–$160/m² for superstructure only

Labour Market Conditions

The GTA's unionized construction labour market (LiUNA Local 183, OPCMIA Local 18 for concrete; UA Local 46 for ironworkers) means labour costs are governed by collective agreements with scheduled annual increases. ICI (Industrial, Commercial, Institutional) concrete labour runs approximately $65–$90/hr all-in. Structural steel erection is $75–$110/hr all-in. Labour market tightness during peak construction periods (spring–fall) creates premium conditions and extended schedules.

Material Price Volatility

Ontario structural material costs are exposed to significant market volatility. Ready-mix concrete prices in Toronto are relatively stable (typically $170–$240/m³ for standard mixes), but rebar (10M–30M) prices fluctuated from $900/tonne to $1,600/tonne between 2020–2024. Structural steel (W-sections) similarly fluctuated between $2,000–$3,800/tonne fabricated and delivered. Forward pricing and escalation clauses in construction contracts are increasingly important risk management tools.

Building Permit Fees & Development Charges

Ontario municipalities charge development charges and building permit fees that can add $40–$120/m² of GFA to project costs in the GTA. Toronto's building permit fees for commercial construction run approximately 1.1% of construction value. Development charges for residential GTA projects can range from $60,000–$130,000 per unit depending on municipality — these directly affect pro forma viability and must be reflected in cost estimates from early feasibility stages.

Escalation, Risk & Contingency

Any credible Ontario construction estimate must explicitly address three sources of financial risk beyond base costs:

  • Design contingency: Uncertainty in the design scope that will increase quantities as design advances. At Class 4 stage, a 15–25% design contingency on structural costs is appropriate.
  • Construction contingency: Allowance for unforeseen site conditions, field changes, and claim resolution. Typically 5–15% of construction cost depending on project type and site condition knowledge.
  • Escalation: Construction cost inflation in Ontario averaged 6–12% annually from 2020–2024. Long-duration projects (24+ months to completion) require explicit escalation provisions, typically modelled using Statistics Canada construction cost indexes and forward curves for key commodities.

Owners and lenders who receive estimates without explicitly stated contingency and escalation assumptions are receiving incomplete information. Asvakas Engineering's cost estimates always state the estimate class, base date, assumptions, exclusions, and all contingency and escalation provisions in the report.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a structural quantity takeoff?

A quantity takeoff (QTO) is the measurement and calculation of all structural material quantities (concrete, rebar, steel, formwork) from the engineer's drawings. These quantities, combined with current unit costs, produce the structural cost estimate.

How much does structural engineering cost in Ontario?

Fees range from $1,500–$5,000 for simple residential work to $25–$60/m² of GFA for full structural engineering services on a mid-rise building. Forensic and specialty work is typically billed at $175–$350/hr P.Eng. rates. Asvakas Engineering provides fee proposals at no charge.

What is the AACE estimate classification system?

AACE International's five-class system categorizes cost estimates by the level of project definition at the time of preparation, from Class 5 (order-of-magnitude, -50% to +100%) to Class 1 (final construction documents, -5% to +10%). Knowing the class is essential to understanding how much confidence to place in a given estimate.

Can I get a cost estimate before I have full structural drawings?

Yes — Asvakas Engineering can provide Class 4 or Class 3 estimates during schematic or design development phases. These estimates are less accurate than final-document estimates but are valuable for feasibility decisions, lender submissions, and comparing system alternatives before committing to full design.

Should I include HST in my construction cost budget?

HST (13% in Ontario) applies to construction services and materials but may be recoverable as an input tax credit (ITC) for commercial/investment property owners who are HST-registered. Owner-occupied residential construction is not eligible for ITCs and HST is a real cost. Always confirm the HST treatment with your accountant when budgeting construction projects in Ontario.
