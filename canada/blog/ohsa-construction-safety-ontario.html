OHSA Construction Safety Ontario: Structural Engineer's Obligations

OHSA and O. Reg. 213/91 impose direct legal obligations on structural engineers working on Ontario construction projects — from shoring stamp thresholds to designers' duties under Section 30. Here's what every engineer and project owner needs to know.

OHSA Framework: Who Is Responsible for What

The Occupational Health and Safety Act (OHSA) (R.S.O. 1990, c. O.1) is Ontario's primary workplace safety statute. On construction projects, OHSA defines clear roles and responsibilities:

PartyOHSA RoleKey Obligations
Project Owner/Client"Owner"Register the project with Ministry of Labour; notify MOL of project start; ensure constructor compliance
General Contractor"Constructor"Overall responsibility for all workplace health and safety on the project; ensure all employer and worker compliance
Trade Contractors"Employer"Employer's obligations to their workers; provide PPE, safe equipment, trained workers
Structural Engineer"Supervisor" (when directing workers) and "Designer" (under s.30)Employer duties when supervising workers; designer duties regarding construction hazards
Workers"Worker"Follow safe work procedures, use PPE, report hazards

O. Reg. 213/91: Construction Projects Regulation

Ontario Regulation 213/91 (Construction Projects) is the detailed technical regulation under OHSA that applies to all construction projects in Ontario. It covers everything from excavation and shoring to fall protection, cranes, and electrical safety. For structural engineers, the most directly relevant sections are:

  • Part IV — Foundations and Excavations (ss. 222–246): Requirements for excavation safety, slope stability, and shoring design
  • Part VI — Formwork and Falsework (ss. 87–92): Requirements for concrete formwork and supporting falsework
  • Part XII — Structural Work (ss. 148–165): Requirements for steel erection, structural stability, and temporary support during construction

Shoring Design: When PE Stamp Is Required

O. Reg. 213/91 requires drawings prepared and stamped by a Professional Engineer for shoring systems in the following situations:

  • Trenches or excavations deeper than 6 metres — PE-stamped design mandatory regardless of system type
  • Any shoring adjacent to or supporting an existing structure — regardless of depth
  • Any shoring not conforming to a standard type for which generic PE-stamped designs have been pre-published (rare in practice; most urban excavations require site-specific design)
  • Any bracing or support of an existing building during renovation, alteration, or demolition
⚠️

Critical threshold: For excavations less than 6 metres deep, the regulation prescribes specific geometric requirements for cut slopes and standard benching configurations that do not require a PE stamp — but these generic provisions assume homogeneous soil conditions and no adjacent structures. In Toronto's urban core, adjacent building foundations and service infrastructure typically require a site-specific PE-designed shoring system regardless of depth.

Formwork Engineering Requirements

O. Reg. 213/91 requires formwork (including falsework supporting formwork) to be designed by a Professional Engineer when the concrete to be placed:

  • Has a depth exceeding 1.2 metres, or
  • Has a volume exceeding 3 cubic metres, or
  • Is to be placed in shores supporting more than one storey

The PE-stamped formwork drawings must include: design loads, component sizes and spacing, shoring layout, reshoring requirements for multi-story pours, and maximum concrete pour height and rate constraints. These drawings must be on-site throughout the forming and pouring operations.

The structural engineer authorizes formwork stripping based on design-specified criteria — typically concrete cylinder break results confirming the slab has reached a specified percentage of design strength (commonly 75% of f'c), or through the use of a maturity-based strength assessment method.

Trench and Excavation Requirements

Below-grade work in Ontario must comply with O. Reg. 213/91 Part IV. Key requirements:

  • Excavations 1.2 m or deeper must be sloped to a stable angle, benched, or shored
  • No worker permitted in an unsupported trench deeper than 1.2 m unless the walls are sloped at a safe angle specified in the Regulation based on soil type
  • Before any worker enters an excavation, ground conditions must be assessed by a competent worker
  • Water accumulation in excavations must address to prevent soil softening and wall collapse
  • Excavated soil must be deposited at least 1.0 m from the edge of the excavation (or farther as required by stability analysis)

Structural Stability of Existing Buildings During Construction

O. Reg. 213/91 Part XII requires that during construction, alteration, or demolition, the structural stability of existing structures must be maintained at all times. Specific requirements:

  • Before any structural member is removed or altered, the method of maintaining structural stability must be determined and implemented
  • A PE must design any temporary bracing, shoring, or support system used to maintain stability of an existing structure during alteration or demolition
  • No load may be applied to a floor, roof, or other structure in excess of its permitted loading at any time during construction (no overstacking of materials)

Section 30: Designer's Duties

OHSA Section 30 imposes specific duties on designers of structures. A "designer" includes any person who designs a building, structure, or plant for a project in Ontario — which explicitly includes structural engineers who design or stamp structural drawings. Section 30(1) requires that the designer:

  1. Consult with a constructor (i.e., discuss with the general contractor or a representative) in the design phase about whether construction will create or be likely to create a hazard to a worker
  2. Take the results of that consultation into account in completing the design

This requirement applies to the structural engineer as a designer. In practice, it means engineers should engage in constructability reviews, consider temporary conditions during construction (shoring, erection, forming), and document their consideration of construction-phase hazards. The Ministry of Labour has issued guidance that the consultation does not need to be formal — a design team meeting with the GC where construction methods are discussed can satisfy this requirement if documented.

Concept Safety Plan

For large, complex, or high-risk projects, designers prepare a Concept Safety Plan as part of their Section 30 compliance documentation. The Plan:

  • Identifies specific construction-phase hazards created by the structural or architectural design
  • Recommends mitigation measures for the constructor to address
  • Documents the designer's consideration of worker safety during design
  • Is typically incorporated into the project's overall Safety Plan by the constructor

Concept Safety Plans are not universally required for all Ontario projects — but for deep excavations, tunnels, buildings with complex temporary works requirements, and any project where the design creates obvious construction-phase hazards, preparing one is both best practice and evidence of due diligence.

Engineer as Supervisor Under OHSA

When a structural engineer is present on-site and directs workers — even temporarily — they may be classified as a "supervisor" under OHSA. Supervisors have specific duties under Section 27, including ensuring workers comply with the Act and are aware of hazards, using PPE, and following safe work procedures. Engineers should be aware of this potential supervisory role and its legal implications whenever they are physically directing construction operations on an Ontario site.

Due Diligence Defense

The OHSA "due diligence" defense allows a party to avoid liability for a safety violation if they can demonstrate that all reasonable precautions were taken in the circumstances. For structural engineers, demonstrating due diligence in Ontario typically involves:

  • PE-stamped design drawings for all shoring and formwork systems above thresholds
  • Pre-pour inspection of formwork by the engineer or designate before concrete placement
  • Field review notes documenting conditions observed and any compliance issues flagged
  • Written correspondence when safety concerns are identified — don't rely on verbal direction
  • Section 30 consultation documentation, including any Concept Safety Plan prepared

Frequently Asked Questions

What are an Ontario structural engineer's obligations under OHSA?

Under OHSA and O. Reg. 213/91, Ontario structural engineers must: design PE-stamped shoring and formwork systems above prescribed thresholds; comply with Section 30 designer's duties by consulting with the constructor about construction-phase hazards; and when directing workers on-site, fulfill the duties of a supervisor under OHSA Section 27. Failure to fulfill these duties creates legal and professional liability exposure.

When is a Professional Engineer stamp required for shoring in Ontario?

A PE stamp is required for: any shoring in excavations deeper than 6 metres; any shoring adjacent to or supporting an existing structure; any non-standard shoring system; and any bracing or support of an existing building during alteration or demolition. In Toronto's urban dense environment, nearly all commercial excavations require site-specific PE-designed shoring regardless of depth.

What is a formwork engineer's role in Ontario?

The formwork engineer designs the falsework system to safely support concrete placement loads (dead load + live load of construction). PE-stamped drawings are required for pours exceeding 1.2 m depth, 3 m³ volume, or multi-story shoring. The drawings govern the falsework plan on-site, and the engineer authorizes stripping based on concrete strength test results.

What are the OHSA duties of a structural engineer as project designer?

OHSA Section 30 requires structural engineers (as designers) to consult with the constructor about construction-phase hazards created by the design, and to take those results into account in completing the design. This obligation must be documented. For complex projects, a Concept Safety Plan should be prepared to illustrate what construction-phase hazards were considered and what the designer recommends as mitigation.

What is an OHSA Concept Safety Plan and when is it required?

A Concept Safety Plan documents the designer's Section 30 analysis — identifying construction hazards created by the design and recommending mitigation measures. It is required for designated complex projects and is best practice for projects involving deep excavations, unusual structural systems, or any design that creates a non-obvious construction-phase hazard. It forms part of the constructor's overall safety plan.
